# Introduction

This is an instructional guide for the RetroCultMods Revival Kit for the Xbox 360 Gibson Xplorer. This build can be completed in under an hour and a half, but can take longer if you are thorough with the cleaning process. If you run into any issues during the build or have questions, [click here!](https://retrocultmods.freshdesk.com/support/tickets/new)

## Parts Included

- Revival Kit Zeroboard (Strumboard/PCB)
- Revival Kit Fretboard
- 3D Printed Translucent Strumbar for Kramer
- 5x 3D Printed Translucent Frets
- 3D Printed Kit Hardware
- Replacement Start/Select Board for Kramer
- Detachable USB-C Board and bracket
- New Whammy Assembly
- Switch Puller
- Color Coded Wires and Auth Cable
- 10 foot nylon braided USB-A to USB-C cable
- Tube of lubricant

## 1. Opening the Guitar

Flip the controller over so the **Red Octane** sticker on the back of the body is visible. The type of screws used on the outside shells will depend on the model of your guitar:\\

Ensure that **all** screws are removed, and open the shell.\
\
Once the shell begins to open, slowly move down the rest of the shell until it pops open completely.\
Repeat this process for the neck, using the opening at the neck connector and working towards the headstock.

If the shell seems to gets stuck, **DO NOT FORCE IT**. Check to make sure you have removed **all** of the screws before continuing to pry the shell.

## 3. (Optional) Clean Your Guitar

In this step, I took a little bit of time to clean some parts of the guitar. I suggest being more thorough in your build, as your guitar may have finger grime from way back in the day!

A bit of rubbing alcohol and/or soap and water can go a long way. Make sure the parts are fully dry if you clean with soap and water.

Pro tip: Toothbrush, Dawn soap, and water.

## 9. Assembling and Installing the Whammy

This part is a little complicated. We suggest watching the video along with following the guide.

<Steps>
  <Step>
    Gather the whammy potentiometer, 3D printed whammy assembly, 2x spring arms, whammy spring, main whammy pivot (with bar attached), and white whammy frame piece
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Starting with the main whammy pivot, attach the one of the spring arms to the post on the side with the blocker piece sticking out. Pictured below:
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Put the spring onto the hook on the both spring arms, then put the other spring arm onto the post. It should be able to hold itself in place and not fall off. It should look like this:
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Moving onto the whammy assembly, we need to determine which assembly pieces we need.

    There are two sets of assemblies, one for left mounted whammies and one for right mounted whammies. You can determine which side you need by the original potentiometer's location. This graphic can show you which side to pick:
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Now that you've figured out which side you need, separate the two halves of the assemblies. They are labeled with a R and L. Now, insert the new whammy potentiometer into the bottom assembly piece. We'll come back to the top assembly piece later.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Insert the whammy potentiometer's turning pin into the main whammy pivot on the side you installed the spring arms. The 3D printed whammy assembly piece holding the potentiometer will feel loose. This is intentional.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Now take the white whammy frame piece and insert it onto the other side of the main whammy pivot. The side that is closer to the post should be matching height with the 3D printed assembly we've already installed.

## 13. Closing the Guitar

You’re almost done! Once you have ensured that each input works, we can close up the guitar!

Make sure none of the wires are in the way of any plastic standoffs, as this will damage them when closing the shell. I would recommend taping down the wires to ensure they are out of the way and not draped across any of the boards.

With the front body and neck shell facing down, put the rear neck shell onto the front neck shell. You may need to apply pressure to close the shell. If you feel like part of the shell will not snap into place, open it up and double check that there is nothing blocking the standoffs. Repeat for the body shell.

**DON’T FORGET THE STRAP PEGS!**

  </Step>

    <img

### Whammy

<Steps>
  <Step>
    Open RCM Programming Tool if you do not already have it open
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Scroll to the Whammy section. Click on it to open the dropdown menu, then click Calibrate.

    <img
      src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/RCMLaunch/docs/refs/heads/main/images/Programmer/CalibrateButton.png"
      className="mx-auto"
      style={{ width:"25%" }}
    />
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Leave the whammy in it’s resting position (as in don’t press it down). Click Next.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Press the whammy down into its lowest position until it comes to a stopping point. Hold it there and click Next. You can release the whammy bar now.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Now you’re setting the Deadzone. Push the whammy bar down ever so slightly to observe the value moving around. You want it to have a little bit of force required before the whammy begins activating. Once you’ve found the sweet spot for your guitar, hold it there and click Next.

    <img
      src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/RCMLaunch/docs/refs/heads/main/images/Programmer/whammy%20deadzone.png"
      className="mx-auto"
      style={{ width:"66%" }}
    />
  </Step>
  <Step>
    You’re done calibrating! You’ll see the Calibrated Value go up and down and you press your whammy. What you want is for the Calibrated Value to not display anything when you’re not touching the whammy bar.
  </Step>
</Steps>
